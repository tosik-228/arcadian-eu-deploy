The problem

The most regulated corner of EU construction runs on Excel

Subcontracting chains carry legal obligations at every link. Getting them wrong is not an inconvenience — it is joint liability, blocked payments and fines.

Chain liability (30bis)

Pay a subcontractor with social-security or tax debts without withholding, and the debt can become yours. Every invoice requires a debt check and the correct retention.

Presence registration

Checkin@work presence registration on regulated job sites — per worker, per day, across companies that share nothing but the site address.

Posted workers

Cross-border crews need A1 and LIMOSA declarations that match reality on site. Enforcement is tightening across the EU, and inspections happen on the werf, not in the office.

Distribution

The chain onboards itself

Werfvolt is not sold seat by seat. A client onboards its subcontractors, because it needs their compliance status before anyone sets foot on site. Each subcontractor brings its own crews — and its other clients.

Every new organisation joins with its counterparties already on the platform. Distribution is a property of the product, not a sales team.

Why now

Regulation is the tailwind

Three forces push the whole market onto software at once.

E-invoicing is mandatory

Structured Peppol e-invoicing between Belgian businesses is required as of 2026. Paper and PDF invoices are no longer an option — every subcontractor needs tooling.

Enforcement is tightening

Site inspections, presence registration and posted-worker rules are converging across the EU. The cost of informal administration keeps rising.

Chains are getting longer

Labour shortage pushes construction toward cross-border subcontracting. More layers, more languages, more compliance surface — more need for one system of record.
